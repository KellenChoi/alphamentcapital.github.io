Algorithmic Trading Strategies

by Kellen Choi

Posted on Feb 28, 2019

Algorithmic trading is a set of trading instructions or rules which are then used on exchanges to automate execution of orders without human intervention. In this articel, we will talk about classification of algorithmic trading strategies used in automated trading and modelling ideas.

"All the " algorithmic trading strategies "that are being used today can be classified broadly into the following categories:"

    • Momentum-based Strategies or Trend Following Algorithmic Trading Strategies
    • Arbitrage Algorithmic Trading Strategies
    • Statistical Arbitrage Algorithmic Trading Strategies
    • Market Making Algorithmic Trading Strategies

Momentum-based Strategies

Momentum-based strategies is to follow a particular trend in the market by using statistics to determine if that trend is going to continue or it will change in the coming weeks. Momentum Strategies seek to profit from the continuance of the existing trend by taking advantage of market swings. “In simple words, buy high and sell higher and vice versa."

In this particular algorithmic trading strategy we will take short-term positions in stocks that are going up or down until they show signs of reversal. It is counter-intuitive to almost all other well-known strategies. Momentum investing is based on the gap in time before mean reversion occurs and chasing performance , but in a systematic way taking advantage of other performance chasers who are making emotional decisions. Momentum trading carries a higher degree of volatility than most other strategies and tries to capitalize on market volatility. It is important to time the buys and sells correctly to avoid losses by using proper risk management techniques and stop losses. Momentum investing requires proper monitoring and appropriate diversification to safeguard against such severe crashes.

Modelling ideas of Momentum-based strategies is firstly to detect Price momentum or Earnings momentum . Price momentum can be detected by following stocks and ETFs that have been continuously going up for days, weeks or even several months in a row. For instance, identify the stocks trading within 10% of their 52 weeks high or look at the percentage price change over the last 12 or 24 weeks. Similarly to spot a shorter trend, include a shorter term price change. Earnings momentum is to look at earnings to understand the movements in stock prices to exploit market under-reaction to information related to short-term earnings.

    • Price Momentum Strategies: A price momentum strategy may profit from the market’s slow response to a broader set of information including longer-term profitability.
    • Earnings Momentum Strategies: An earnings momentum strategy may profit from the under-reaction to information related to short-term earnings.

Arbitrage Strategies

Arbitrage strategies is triggered by corporate events. For example, if we assume that a pharma-corp is to be bought by another company, then the stock price of that corp could go up. It can be called an event-driven strategy where you believe there are pricing inefficiencies that many happen during a corporate event.

As other corporate actions there are bankruptcy, acquisition, merger, spin-offs etc. They could be the event that drives such kind of an investment strategy. These arbitrage trading strategies can be market neutral and used by hedge funds and proprietary traders widely.

Statistical Arbitrage Strategies

Statistical Arbitrageseeks to profit from statistical mispricing of one or more assets based on the expected value of these assets. Catching an arbitrage opportunity due to misquoting in price is the best use of algorithmic trading strategies, as an automated machine can track such changes instantly. For instance, if Apple‘s price falls under $1 then Microsoft will fall by $ 0.5 but Microsoft has not fallen, so you will go and sell Microsoft to make a profit.

Mostly,Statistical Arbitrage Algorithms are based on mean reversion hypothesis, mostly as a pair. Pairs trading is one of the several strategies collectively referred to as Statistical Arbitrage Strategies. In pairs trade strategy, stocks that exhibit historical co-movement in prices are paired using fundamental or market-based similarities. The strategy builds upon the notion that the relative prices in a market are in equilibrium, and that deviations from this equilibrium eventually will be corrected.
